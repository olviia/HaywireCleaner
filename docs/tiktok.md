● Your instinct is right — it doesn't sound full yet. Here's why: what you have is a situation ("bot
has a virus") plus a design question ("glitch or text?"). A story needs a protagonist who wants
something and hits obstacles. But here's the good news — you already have the story, you just filed
it under "extra": the reference search IS the story. You, hunting for the answer, is the narrative.
The virus is just the setting.

Also, one hook-level fix: "why a game about a vacuum cleaner can be interesting" is a defensive hook
— it asks the viewer to give you a chance. Lead with the twist instead, because your premise is
genuinely strong: "You're not playing the robot. You're playing the virus." That's a hook that needs
no apology.

Now the wiring, beat by beat:

1. Hook (you, on camera): "In my game you play a computer virus that possesses cleaning robots. And
   I have a problem: how do I make the player feel the moment of infection?" — that opens two loops at
   once (cool premise + unsolved problem).
2. The bad option, played straight: cut to a mockup with flat text: YOU GOT A VIRUS. Hold it for a  
   beat. Let it be funny-bad. This is your "obstacle" beat — telling isn't showing, and the viewer     
   instantly feels why it fails. You don't have to explain it.
3. The search (your reference hunt, screen-recorded): scrolling, pausing on finds. This is where the
   personal brand lives — dev-as-detective. Two or three references max, named: Cyberpunk 2077's relic
   malfunction glitch (the overlay you mentioned), and I'd strongly suggest Eternal Darkness' sanity   
   effects — the canonical "the game lies to you through its own UI" reference — ~~or Arkham Asylum's    
   fake crash-to-desktop~~.
4. The payoff — the insight, not the solution: what all those references share: the effect isn't    
   drawn on top of the screen, it corrupts the interface the player already trusts. The UI itself      
   becomes the character. That's a real principle (diegetic UI), and stating it makes you sound like   
   someone worth following — you didn't pick an effect, you found a rule.
5. End — throw the open loop to the audience: "So: glitch, corrupted text, or something worse? I'm  
   building it next week — comments decide." Hard cut.

Notice what this does with your worry that it's unresolved: the unresolved question is an asset, not
a gap. Genuine questions get comments, comments drive reach, and it pre-sells part 2 — the video    
where you show what you built. You're serializing the open loop across videos, which is exactly how
devlog creators build a following.
